# 📚 SAT Punctuation Mastery — Study Guide

## 🎯 Key Concepts
*   **Clause Connection:** Punctuation marks like commas, semicolons, and colons are essential for correctly joining or separating independent and dependent clauses within sentences, especially in compound and complex structures.
*   **Additional Information:** Commas, dashes, and parentheses are used to set off non-essential or additional information that can be removed from a sentence without changing its core meaning.
*   **Lists and Emphasis:** Colons and commas primarily introduce lists, while colons and dashes can also provide emphasis or introduce an explanation of a preceding statement.
*   **Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Information:** The choice between 'that' and ',which' depends on whether the clause provides essential (restrictive) or non-essential (non-restrictive) information.

## 📋 Essential Rules / Facts
1.  **Comma (,) for Lists:** Use a comma to separate three or more elements (X, Y, and Z), including before 'and'.
2.  **Comma (,) for Compound Sentences:** Place a comma before coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So) when connecting two independent clauses (S+V).
3.  **Comma (,) for Complex Sentences:** When a dependent clause comes first in a complex sentence, place a comma after it and before the independent clause.
4.  **Comma (,) for Additional Information:** Use commas to set off additional, non-essential information or explanations that can be removed from the sentence.
5.  **Comma (,) vs. 'that':** Use ',which' for non-essential (removable) information, and 'that' without commas for essential (non-removable) information.
6.  **Comma (,) for Adjectives:** Use a comma between two adjectives if 'and' can be placed between them.
7.  **Colon (:) for Lists:** Use a colon to introduce a list of items.
8.  **Colon (:) for Explanation:** Place a colon between two clauses when the second clause explains the first, or can be paraphrased with "because."
9.  **Colon (:) for Emphasis:** A colon can be used to provide emphasis after a preceding statement.
10. **Semicolon (;) for Independent Clauses:** Use a semicolon between two independent clauses when there is no conjunction connecting them.
11. **Semicolon (;) with Transitional Expressions:** Use a semicolon between two independent clauses linked by a transitional expression (e.g., however, furthermore, nevertheless).
12. **Semicolon (;) in Complex Lists:** Use semicolons to separate items in a list that themselves contain internal commas.
13. **Dash (–) for Emphasis:** A dash can be used to provide emphasis, similar to a colon.
14. **Dash (–) for Additional Information:** Use dashes to set off additional information or explanation, similar to commas or parentheses.

## 💡 Common Pitfalls
*   **Confusing 'that' and ',which':** Using ',which' for essential information that should be introduced by 'that' (without commas), or vice versa. Remember that ',which' clauses are always non-essential.
*   **Missing Commas with FANBOYS:** Forgetting to place a comma before FANBOYS when they connect two complete independent clauses.
*   **Incorrect Comma after Dependent Clause:** Failing to use a comma when a dependent clause begins a sentence and precedes the independent clause.
*   **Misusing Semicolons:** Using a semicolon where a comma with a FANBOYS conjunction is needed, or using it to introduce a list (which requires a colon).
*   **Overusing Commas:** Placing commas around essential information that should not be set off.
*   **Interchanging Colon and Dash for Emphasis:** While both can emphasize, not understanding the subtle difference where a colon often introduces an explanation and a dash is for a more abrupt break or afterthought.

## ✅ Quick Checks
1.  Identify the correct punctuation: "The artist specializes in three mediums____ oil, watercolor, and acrylic."
2.  Determine if a comma is needed: "When the bell rings____ the students will leave the classroom."
3.  Choose the correct punctuation: "My car____ that is always breaking down____ needs to be replaced."
4.  Which punctuation mark can be used to separate two independent clauses without a conjunction?
5.  Identify the sentence where the comma is used correctly:
    a) I enjoy running, reading, and watching movies.
    b) I enjoy running reading, and watching movies.

## 🎓 Pro Tips
*   **Master Clause Identification:** The ability to consistently identify independent and dependent clauses is fundamental. Practice breaking down sentences into their subject-verb pairs to understand their relationships.
*   **Test for Removability:** For information set off by commas, dashes, or parentheses, mentally remove the phrase. If the sentence still makes complete sense, the punctuation is likely correct for additional information.
*   **Understand the "Why":** Don't just memorize rules; understand the *purpose* behind each punctuation mark. Does it add information, provide an explanation, emphasize, or separate? This conceptual understanding will help you apply rules more flexibly.
